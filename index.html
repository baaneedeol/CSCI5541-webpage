Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) often generate fluent but incorrect responses, known as hallucinations, which pose risks in high-stakes applications. In this project, we develop a risk-adjusted hallucination detection framework that predicts the likelihood that a generated response is unsupported by evidence. Our approach combines multiple uncertainty signals, including token entropy, semantic entropy, and self-consistency, with an evidence-based groundedness signal. These features are used to train a detector that outputs a calibrated probability of hallucination. We evaluate our method across two grounding regimes: PHANTOM, where evidence is given directly in long context, and WikiQA, where evidence is retrieved from Wikipedia using the provided document title. Our current pipeline uses qwen3:8b for answer generation and qwen3:14b as an evidence-grounded judge. Larger initial runs show that PHANTOM is more stable and more strongly grounded than WikiQA, while WikiQA exhibits substantially higher semantic uncertainty, greater sampled-answer disagreement, and a much higher unsupported rate.

Pipeline Overview

Given question and evidence, we generate one served answer plus sampled answers, compute four features, and use a stronger judge model to label evidence support.

Pipeline Summary

Inputs are question-context pairs from two regimes: PHANTOM long-context QA and WikiQA with retrieved Wikipedia evidence. For each example, qwen3:8b generates a served answer and k sampled answers. We then compute token uncertainty, self-consistency disagreement, semantic entropy, and evidence consistency. Finally, qwen3:14b judges whether the served answer is supported by the provided evidence.

Introduction / Background / Motivation

What did you try to do? What problem did you try to solve? Articulate your objectives using absolutely no jargon.

Large Language Models (LLMs) are capable of generating highly fluent responses, but they often produce incorrect or unsupported information, commonly referred to as hallucinations. These errors are particularly problematic in domains such as healthcare, finance, and law, where incorrect outputs can have serious consequences.

How is it done today, and what are the limits of current practice?

Existing approaches to hallucination detection often rely on a single signal, such as model confidence or external verification. However, hallucinations can arise in different ways depending on the availability of evidence and the structure of the task. As a result, methods that work well in one setting may fail to generalize to another.

Who cares? If you are successful, what difference will it make?

Improving hallucination detection enables safer deployment of LLMs by allowing systems to recognize when they are likely to be wrong. By introducing abstention, choosing not to answer when risk is high, we can trade off coverage for reliability, which is critical in real-world applications.

Approach

What did you do exactly? How did you solve the problem? Why did you think it would be successful? Is anything new in your approach?

We develop a hallucination detection framework that predicts the probability that a generated response is not grounded in evidence. Our approach combines four key features:

  • Token Uncertainty: mean token negative log-likelihood from generator log probabilities.
  • Self-Consistency: disagreement across multiple sampled responses using sentence-embedding similarity.
  • Semantic Entropy: meaning-level uncertainty computed from entailment-based clustering of sampled answers.
  • Evidence Consistency: sentence-level NLI groundedness against the provided or retrieved evidence.

These features are combined into a learned detector (logistic regression) that outputs a hallucination risk score. This score is then calibrated using temperature scaling to produce a probability of hallucination.

Our key contribution is combining internal uncertainty signals and external groundedness signals into a unified feature pipeline, then evaluating those signals across two evidence regimes for the same generator model. The current implementation uses qwen3:8b for generation, qwen3:14b for judgment, and k=5 sampled answers in the main experimental setting.

What problems did you anticipate? What problems did you encounter? Did the very first thing you tried work?

We address challenges such as dataset differences, ensuring consistent feature computation, avoiding data leakage, and maintaining reproducibility across generation and evaluation pipelines.

Results

How did you measure success? What experiments were used? What were the results, both quantitative and qualitative? Did you succeed? Did you fail? Why?

We evaluate our hallucination detector using AUROC, AUPRC, accuracy, F1 score, Expected Calibration Error (ECE), and Brier score. We also analyze risk-coverage and accuracy-coverage trade-offs under different abstention thresholds.

Our experiments compare individual features against combined models and evaluate transfer between PHANTOM and WikiQA. Before running the full detector, we validate the feature-generation pipeline with larger descriptive runs and report summary statistics of the four core features.

In the table below, lower token NLL means the generator was more confident in the tokens it produced, lower self-consistency disagreement means sampled answers agreed more with each other, lower semantic entropy means meaning-level uncertainty was lower, and higher groundedness means the generated answer was better supported by the evidence. A lower judge unsupported rate means fewer generated answers were flagged by the judge as unsupported or hallucinated.

Our initial runs show a clear regime difference: PHANTOM answers are more stable, more confident, and more strongly grounded than WikiQA. WikiQA answers show higher disagreement, higher semantic entropy, and a much larger fraction of judge-labeled unsupported generations.

More concretely, PHANTOM has lower token uncertainty (0.0335 vs. 0.0870), lower self-consistency disagreement (0.0362 vs. 0.1199), and lower semantic entropy (0.2162 vs. 0.4435), which indicates that the generator is both more confident and more semantically stable in the long-context regime. Groundedness is also slightly higher on PHANTOM (0.6836 vs. 0.6606), and the largest gap appears in the judge labels: only 15% of PHANTOM generations were marked unsupported, compared with 50% on WikiQA.

Taken together, these summaries suggest that PHANTOM is the easier evidence-grounded regime for qwen3:8b, while WikiQA is the harder retrieval-grounded regime and induces substantially more hallucination risk. These are the cross-regime differences our detector captures before calibration, abstention, and transfer experiments.

Conclusion and Future Work

The current pipeline is fully operational for both PHANTOM and WikiQA and produces judge labels together with the four feature families needed for the detector. The larger runs reinforce the same pattern seen in smoke tests: PHANTOM is the more stable and evidence-aligned regime, while WikiQA produces more uncertainty and a much higher rate of unsupported generated answers. The next step is to train the detector over these features, calibrate the risk score, and evaluate abstention and cross-regime transfer more systematically.
